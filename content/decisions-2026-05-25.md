## Strategic implications

### From "selective fundraising" to "movement fundraising"
Barrett's #4 answer is the most important strategic signal. He's NOT building a 5-to-10-anchor-donor model. He's building a **movement model**: thousands of small donors → community-wide ownership → political legitimacy.

This is the right call. Here's why:
- The plebiscite requires **3,500 signatures from registered voters**. Every donor is a potential signer.
- The plebiscite requires **2/3 YES vote of the residents of the proposed territory**. Every donor is a vote.
- The 2/3 BCS Congress vote is influenced by **demonstrated community mandate**. 2,000 donors at 50 pesos says more than 5 donors at 200K.
- Open-tent fundraising kills the "wealthy outsider land-grab" narrative before La Paz cabildo can weaponize it.

### Stage I as the proof-of-concept fundraise
- **Target: 600K MXN ($35K USD) in 2 months.** That's a feasible, story-driven micro-campaign.
- If we can't raise 600K from the community, the plebiscite won't pass anyway. So Stage I funding IS the first signal of viability.
- 300K NOW = today's urgency. The remaining 300K can come during the 2-month window as the Stage I work itself generates momentum (survey results, boundary map, committee formation announcements).

### "Other ways to support" tracks
For donors who can't give money:
- **Signature gatherers** — direct path to the 3,500-signature gate
- **Promoter Committee volunteers** — Vita's Stage I deliverable #1 needs this body
- **Community advisors** — water, infrastructure, tourism, agriculture, cultural perspectives
- **Local hosts** — house meetings, town halls, listening sessions
- **Translators** — Spanish ↔ English content adaptation
- **Skill donors** — design, video, social media, legal review, accounting
- **Vouches** — community leaders willing to be named publicly as supporters
