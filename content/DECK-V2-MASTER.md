## Strategic posture (read before reading the deck)

This deck does three things and only three things:

1. **Reframe the question** from "should we leave La Paz?" to "what's the right size of community to make the decisions that affect our daily lives?"
2. **Surface the real cost** of the current structure (water, power, development planning, response times) using local pain points everyone already feels.
3. **Open the door** to a community-led process — not a developer-led one — by naming a Founding Committee and committing publicly to transparency, stage-gating, and resident veto.

It does NOT:
- Ask for money (donor package is a separate document)
- Predict the outcome (we don't know if 2/3 will vote yes, and saying so destroys trust)
- Promise anything (we offer a process, not a result)
- Hide who's behind it (anonymous = dead on arrival in a tight community)

### Slide 7 — WHAT THE LAW ACTUALLY ALLOWS
**Title:** *"The Legal Pathway — What Mexican Law Says"*

**Body copy:**
Mexican federal law and the BCS State Constitution **explicitly allow** the creation of new municipalities through a citizen-initiated process. This is not a workaround. It's a legal right.

**The three legal gates:**

✓ **Gate 1 — Citizen Petition**
*Constitución Política del Estado de BCS, Art. 122; Ley de Participación Ciudadana, Art. 19*
At least **33% of registered voters** in the proposed territory must sign a formal petition. Each signature must include voter ID key (clave de elector).

✓ **Gate 2 — Plebiscite (Public Vote)**
*Constitución Política del Estado de BCS, Art. 122.VIII*
**At least 2/3 of residents who vote** in the plebiscite must vote YES.

✓ **Gate 3 — State Congress**
*Constitución Política del Estado de BCS, Art. 64.XXXV*
**At least 2/3 of BCS State Deputies** must vote YES on the constitutional reform creating the new municipality.

**Plus baseline tests:** minimum 5,000 inhabitants in proposed territory, minimum 3,000 in cabecera city, adequate public services, viable economic base.

**Honest framing:** *"These gates are high — and intentionally so. They make sure no new municipality is created without genuine, demonstrated community support. If we can't pass these gates, we shouldn't be creating a new municipality."*

### Slide 8 — WHAT IT WOULD COST, TRANSPARENTLY
**Title:** *"What This Process Would Cost — Every Peso, Public"*

**Body copy:**
A proper municipalization process in BCS — done right, with technical rigor, community input, and legal grounding — costs approximately **10 million pesos over 20 months**.

**Breakdown by stage:**

| Stage | What It Funds | Cost (MXN) | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I. Foundation** | Promoter Committee, public opinion survey (600 questionnaires), boundary cartographic study | **600,000** | 2 months |
| **II. Plebiscite** | Information campaign, signature collection (3,500+), formal filing, plebiscite mobilization | **5,000,000** | 6 months |
| **III. Legislative & Transition** | State Congress process, de-incorporation, transitional planning, municipal development plan | **2,700,000 base** + 1–3M lobbying | 12 months |
| | **Total range** | **~9.3M – 11.3M** | **20 months** |

**Three public commitments:**

🔒 **Stage-gated funding.** We don't raise Stage II until Stage I deliverables are publicly published. If the community decides not to continue after Stage I, we stop.

🔒 **Open books.** Every peso raised and every peso spent is published quarterly on the PARA TODOS A.C. website. Independent audit at the end of each stage.

🔒 **Community veto.** At any point during the process, if community sentiment turns against continuation, the Founding Committee will pause and consult publicly before deciding whether to proceed.

### Slide 10 — A REGIONAL EXAMPLE
**Title:** *"What's Possible — Looking at Los Cabos"*

**Body copy:**
In 1992, Los Cabos became its own municipality, separated from La Paz. The community pursued a similar legal pathway. Since then:

**Three numbers:**
- **$7.7B USD** annual economic output (2024)
- **~350,000** annual visitors
- **130%** tourism growth over the past decade

**Honest framing:**
This is not a promise. Los Cabos had different geography, different timing, different leadership, and different luck.

**What it does show:**
- That the legal pathway works
- That a coastal community in BCS can self-govern
- That local control over development decisions can produce significant economic outcomes
- That community character can survive and adapt under local governance (Los Cabos retains strong local identity even with rapid growth)

**What it also shows (honest):**
- Rapid growth has costs (traffic, housing pressure, character shifts)
- Not every Los Cabos resident would say it went perfectly
- Our region has the chance to learn from both their wins and their lessons

## Production notes

### Visual style guide
- **Primary palette:** Pacific blues + warm sand + Cerritos-sunset coral. NOT corporate / nonprofit-template green. Feels like the place.
- **Typography:** Modern serif headings (e.g. Fraunces or Cardo) + clean sans body (e.g. Inter or DM Sans). Bilingual presentation friendly.
- **Photography:** Local, real, recognizable. NOT stock. Punta Lobos surf, Pescadero farms, Todos Santos pueblo streets, Cerritos beach, La Esquina coffee shop, ranchero portraits with permission. Photo credit on each image.
- **No people without permission.** Every face in the deck is a real local person who has signed a release.
- **Bilingual layout:** Spanish primary text in larger weight, English secondary in lighter weight on same slide where possible. Or two-version files. Decide with Founding Committee.

### Voice & tone rules
- **Never use "we will" about an outcome we don't control** (e.g., never "we will become a new municipality")
- **Always use "if the community decides"** as conditional framing
- **Avoid:** "breakaway," "secession," "split from," "leave" — too charged. Use: "create," "form," "establish," "explore," "decide."
- **Avoid:** "they" referring to La Paz — too othering. Use: "current structure," "today's framework," "La Paz Municipality."
- **Always credit the law:** when citing legal pathways, cite the specific article. Builds credibility.

### Bilingual considerations
- **Spanish is primary.** Local residents are the primary audience. English-only deck = signals outsider-led.
- **English second.** Expats, second-home owners, snowbirds are critical donors AND signature gatherers.
- **Some communities need pure-Spanish version.** Ranchero communities inland from Todos Santos won't engage with bilingual slides — they need pure ES.
- **Some community sessions need translator present.** Online sessions can use live captioning.

### What's deliberately omitted (and why)
- **No predictions of plebiscite outcome.** Trust killer.
- **No criticism of specific La Paz officials.** Creates enemies.
- **No promises about new municipality services.** We can't guarantee them.
- **No comparison to specific named developers' interests.** Stays issue-focused.
- **No personality cult around any single founder.** Movement framing.
- **No glossy "vision of utopia" renderings.** Stays grounded in real geography + real pain points.
