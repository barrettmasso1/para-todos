San Quintín Did It — And What It Means for Our Region

One of the more recent cases, the most relevant to us, and the lessons that matter most.

This is the third and final part of our series on Mexican communities that became their own municipalities. If you haven't read the previous installments, you can find them on our site. Today we close with one of the more recent cases — and the one whose details most closely mirror the situation of the communities in the Southern Pacific of BCS.

Case 6: San Quintín, Baja California — 2020

San Quintín sits in the southern portion of Baja California Norte, in a broad agricultural valley that opens to the Pacific Ocean. The valley produces strawberries, tomatoes, bell peppers, and other export crops — major contributors to the agricultural economy of northern Mexico and a significant source of the produce that reaches tables in the United States and Canada.

For all of this productivity, San Quintín was, until 2020, a delegación of Ensenada — a distant municipal seat more than 200 kilometers to the north. For any resident of San Quintín who needed a municipal service, a permit, a document, or a resolution to any legal matter, the trip to Ensenada could take six hours round trip. That is not governance. That is abandonment dressed up as administration.

The community had been asking for its own municipality since the early 1990s. The argument was straightforward: San Quintín generates real economic value, has a distinct identity and geography, and deserves a government that is present, accountable, and focused on its specific needs rather than on the priorities of a large, primarily urban, far-northern city.

In 2003, the first formal pro-municipality committee organized. The Comité Pro-Municipalización began building the case — documenting the community's economic contribution, its population, its infrastructure needs, its distance from effective governance.

In 2012, a first citizen consultation was held. Of the votes cast, 17,846 were in favor and 7,402 were against — a clear majority for independence. But the process was structured in a way that required the participation of 10% of the entire Ensenada electoral roll, and organized opposition from Ensenada business interests successfully suppressed turnout. The vote failed on a technicality. Business groups in Ensenada had run a campaign under the slogan "Ensenada juntita se ve más bonita" — "Together, Ensenada looks prettier" — and persuaded enough urban Ensenada residents to stay home that the threshold was not reached.

Then came March 2015.

On March 17, 2015, farmworkers in San Quintín — mostly indigenous migrants from Oaxaca and Guerrero, the backbone of the valley's agricultural workforce — blockaded the Transpeninsular Highway. They stopped production across the valley and presented a 13-point petition that began with denunciations of sexual harassment and child labor and continued with demands for medical care, social security coverage, and fair wages. The protest lasted two months. It made national headlines. It forced federal and state authorities to the negotiating table.

The 2015 strike did not immediately produce a municipality. But it changed the political calculus permanently. San Quintín was no longer a distant agricultural delegación that La Paz and Ensenada could quietly ignore. It was a community with a national profile, a documented history of neglect, and an organized base that had demonstrated its willingness to act.

In 2019, the Morena government of Governor Jaime Bonilla made the municipalization of San Quintín a formal commitment. On February 12, 2020 — gathering in a hotel in San Quintín itself, which was declared a parliamentary venue — the Baja California Congress voted 22 to 0 to create the municipality of San Quintín. Baja California now had six municipalities.

What happened next?

San Quintín now manages its own budget, its own services, and its own administrative apparatus. For the first time in its history, residents can hold their own local authorities directly accountable — not a distant office in Ensenada, but neighbors, people from the valley, who face the same roads and the same schools and the same challenges.

The municipality is not without its early difficulties. Building a functioning government from scratch is genuinely hard. Financial preparation was insufficient going in. But the community today has something it never had before: a voice. And the ability to use it.

What this story teaches us directly

San Quintín's path took seventeen years from the first formal committee to the creation of the municipality. But the actual legislative process — from the formal commitment by the governor to the congressional vote — took less than a year.

The seventeen years were not about the complexity of the process. They were about building the community consensus, finding the right political moment, and surviving the organized opposition of interests that benefited from the status quo.

The formal process, once the conditions were right, was fast.

What We Learned From All Six Cases

After studying all these stories — from Los Cabos in 1981 to San Quintín in 2020 — the pattern is unmistakable.

The cause was always the same. In every single case, the core problem was distance — physical and administrative distance between the community and its governing municipal seat. Money arrived from the state and federal government, but it was managed far away, by people who didn't live in the community, didn't understand its specific needs, and weren't accountable to its residents.

The process is achievable. Some cases took three years. Others took seventeen. Some were driven by federal investment and political will at the top. Others were driven by decades of community organizing from the bottom up. All required a political ally at the state level and a documented case that the community was ready to govern itself.

The results speak for themselves. In every case documented here — without exception — having self-governance meant more local investment, more responsive services, and more ability to shape the community's own future. The money that used to leave started staying. The decisions that used to be made far away started being made close to home.

And Our Region?

The communities of the Southern Pacific of BCS share characteristics with each of these six cases. We generate tourism like Los Cabos. We have our own distinct cultural and geographic identity, like Loreto. We are growing, like Playa del Carmen. We have a unique and fragile natural ecosystem — the Pacific coast, the Sierra de la Laguna, the desert — that requires specific, attentive stewardship, like Bacalar. And like San Quintín, we have already begun to ask questions, to organize, and to demand information.

The question is not whether it is possible. History shows it is.

The question is whether we are ready to inform ourselves, to build the case carefully and thoroughly, and to take the necessary steps — together.

That is why Para Todos exists. Not to tell you what to decide. But to make sure that whatever you decide, you decide with full information.
